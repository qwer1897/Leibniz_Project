The universal genius

One mind.
Many worlds.

Leibniz was a mathematician, but never only a mathematician. He moved between philosophy and law, diplomacy and engineering, always searching for systems that could make complicated thought clearer.

He entered the University of Leipzig in 1661 at just 14, studied philosophy and law, and earned a doctorate in law from the University of Altdorf in 1667. Later, he worked as a political adviser, librarian, diplomat, inventor and writer.

His defining contribution

Calculus,
made legible.

Leibniz independently developed differential and integral calculus around the same time as Isaac Newton. His enduring breakthrough was a notation that made relationships visible—and calculus easier to teach, use and extend.

His biggest contribution

He gave calculus a usable symbolic system.

  • dx and dySymbols for very small changes in x and y.
  • dy/dxNotation for derivatives and the comparison of two changing quantities.
  • ∫The integral sign used for integration.
  • Infinitely small partsThe idea of integration as adding infinitesimal pieces to make a whole.
  • Product ruleA rule for differentiating the product of two functions.
  • Quotient ruleA rule for differentiating one function divided by another.
  • Power ruleThe derivative form d/dx(xⁿ) = nxⁿ⁻¹.

A rule still in use

The power rule

d/dx(xⁿ) = nxⁿ⁻¹

Leibniz developed a form of this rule in 1676. Move the slider to change the exponent.

d/dx(x³) = 3x²

Two functions multiplied

The product rule

(fg)′ = f′g + fg′

Differentiate each factor in turn while leaving the other factor unchanged.

Worked example d/dx (x² · x³) = 2x · x³ + x² · 3x² = 5x⁴

One function divided by another

The quotient rule

(f/g)′ = (f′g − fg′) / g²

Differentiate the top and bottom in a precise order, then divide by the square of the denominator.

Worked example d/dx (x⁴ / x²) = (4x³ · x² − x⁴ · 2x) / (x²)² = 2x

The idea beneath the symbols

Why invent
a new language?

Leibniz believed good notation could make difficult ideas easier to understand—and new discoveries easier to make.

Calculus emerged from his work on curves, tangents, infinitesimals, summation, areas, series, motion and physics. It wasn’t a lucky accident; it was the result of trying to represent change with clarity. He refined his symbols carefully, while Newton’s notation was less consistent and more personal.

VerdictIntentional, not accidental.

Leibniz chose to study mathematical change. Huygens’s guidance deepened that work, and Leibniz deliberately searched for a symbolic system that could solve related problems.
